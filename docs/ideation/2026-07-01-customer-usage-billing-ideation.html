Compound Engineering · Ideation

Billing your end-users, natively

Unkey already holds the four inputs a billing engine needs — the metered event, the identity, the rate-limit counters, and the credit balance. Today customers export all of it and bill out-of-band. These are the strongest directions for closing that last mile inside Unkey.

Codebase Context

Unkey is a Go + TypeScript monorepo (MySQL for operational state, ClickHouse for analytics). The billing landscape breaks into a universal pipeline — meter → aggregate → rate → invoice → collect. Unkey already runs the left half of that pipeline for its own billing; the customer-facing right half does not exist yet.

What Unkey captures. ClickHouse key_verifications_raw_v2 records workspace_id, identity_id, external_id, key_id, outcome, spent_credits, tags and preserves external_id through every per-minute/hour/day/month materialized view. Credits live in MySQL keys.remaining_requests with refill_amount/refill_day and atomic decrement/refill queries.

Two structural gaps. (1) The terminal billable_verifications_per_month_v2 / billable_ratelimits_per_month_v2 tables collapse everything to workspace_id — there is no per-end-user billable rollup. (2) ratelimits_raw_v2 has no external_id/identity_id column at all (only a free-text identifier), so standalone-ratelimit usage — one of the three things the customer named — cannot be attributed to an end-user today.

What exists but points inward. A working Stripe meter pusher (svc/ctrl/internal/billingmeter/stripe.go), a tiered-pricing engine (web/internal/billing/src/tiers.ts), and invoice webhooks all bill Unkey's own customers. There is no Stripe Connect, no customer-facing invoice/usage-export API, no customer-defined rate cards, no customer webhooks. The identities table (the natural billable subject) carries external_id + a meta JSON blob and zero billing fields. The end-user Portal is a placeholder.

External landscape (2026). Every metering vendor — Stripe Billing Meters, Metronome, Orb, Lago, OpenMeter, Amberflo — forces customers to instrument and ship usage events. Zuplo is the closest analog ("the gateway is the meter"). The validated positioning: Unkey uniquely already holds the metered event and the subject mapping, so it can shortcut the ingestion + subject-mapping step everyone else makes customers build. Learnings: RFC 0005 (analytics API with externalId/groupBy=identity) and a usage-billing cookbook that today teaches the DIY pattern.

Topic Axes

  • Metering & aggregation — turning raw verification/ratelimit/credit events into per-end-user billable quantities (per-identity rollups, idempotency, late events, ratelimit attribution).
  • Pricing & rating — what to charge: per-request/tiered/credit-burndown/ratelimit-based models, customer-defined rate cards, markup, currency.
  • Invoicing & collection — producing invoices/charges and moving money: Stripe Connect, invoice records, export, payment rails, dunning.
  • Identity & subject mapping — mapping usage to the customer's end-user/org as a billable subject (identities, external_id, entitlements, per-identity plans).
  • Surfacing & self-serve — how the customer and their end-users see and manage it: dashboards, end-user portal, pricing pages, usage widgets, alerts, webhooks.

The shape of the decision

Ideas 1–2 are the foundation everything else attaches to (a per-end-user rollup, and an identity that can carry billing). Idea 3 is what to charge. Then the real strategic fork: Idea 5 (Unkey hands over data/draft invoices, never touches money) versus Idea 4 (Unkey becomes merchant-of-record and moves the money). Ideas 6–7 make it visible and defensible.

1.

Per-identity usage rollup — the billable-subject data layer

metering & aggregationConfidence 85%Complexity: Medium
Description
Add a billable_usage_per_identity_per_month materialized view keyed on (workspace_id, external_id, year, month) summing verifications, spent_credits, and passed ratelimits — sourced from the per-day views that already preserve external_id. This is the single primitive every other idea depends on: one canonical per-end-user number, computed idempotently, instead of re-scanning raw events at query time. It must also close the ratelimit-attribution gap: ratelimits_raw_v2 has no identity column, so ratelimit usage needs either identity written into the raw event on the hot path, or a rate-time join — a genuine tradeoff (see downsides). The tags Array(String) dimension already flows through the rollup, so per-feature billing (e.g. tag:gpt4) comes almost for free.
Basis
direct: per-day/per-month views GROUP BY external_id/identity_id (pkg/clickhouse/schema/004,005), but billable_verifications_per_month_v2 (019) & billable_ratelimits_per_month_v2 (018) group only by (workspace_id, year, month). ratelimits_raw_v2 (006) indexed by identifier only, no external_id/identity_id. tags Array(String) grouped through per-month view with bloom-filter index. [verifier: sound]
Rationale
The data is already partitioned the right way — the last aggregation hop simply throws the external_id dimension away. Rebuilding it once as an idempotent view means every downstream consumer (rate cards, invoices, portal, webhooks) reads one source of truth instead of re-deriving it, and it is the only thing that makes ratelimit billing possible at all.
Downsides
external_id cardinality can be enormous (a customer's entire end-user base), so the view's storage/query cost and TTL need real design. The ratelimit fix forces a hot-path vs. query-time-join choice, and standalone-ratelimit identifier is an arbitrary caller-supplied string that does not always resolve to an owning key/identity — attribution is not universal.
2.

Identity as a first-class billing subject

identity & subject mappingConfidence 85%Complexity: Medium
Description
Promote the identities record from an auth/ratelimit-scoping object into a billing subject by giving it structured billing fields — a billing_customer_id (the end-user's Stripe customer), a rate_card_id reference, currency, and plan — instead of forcing customers to stuff Stripe IDs into the unstructured 1MB meta JSON and re-parse them. The identity is already the join key across keys, ratelimits, and (via Idea 1) usage; making it also the join to "who pays and what they pay" removes the customer's need to maintain a shadow external_id → stripe_customer reconciliation table.
Basis
direct: identities table (web/internal/db/src/schema/identity.ts) carries only external_id + meta JSON + environment; "No billing metadata fields on identity today: no stripe_id, plan, email, or pricing-tier columns." Workspace is the sole billing subject (workspaces.stripe_customer_id). [verifier: sound — significant data-model decision]
Rationale
Billing is currently hard-wired one level too high (workspace). Every per-end-user idea — rate cards, Connect payouts, statements — needs to resolve an identity to a payer. This is the smallest schema change that makes "point at an end-user and know who to charge" a first-class operation rather than customer-maintained glue.
Downsides
Introduces Unkey's second billing-subject type ever (after workspace), with multi-tenancy and migration implications. The current cookbook pattern ("put Stripe ID in meta") would need a deprecation path. Couples the auth primitive to billing concerns some customers won't use.
3.

Customer-defined rate cards on Unkey's own pricing engine

pricing & ratingConfidence 80%Complexity: Medium
Description
Expose the tiered-pricing engine Unkey already uses to bill itself (tiers.ts: billingTier with firstUnit/lastUnit/centsPerUnit) as a customer-configurable rate card attached to an identity or API, priced on the dimensions Unkey already emits: per-VALID-verification, per-spent_credits, per-passed-ratelimit. Ships with a zero-config default: a "cost-plus" rate card auto-derived from Unkey's own subscription tiers plus a single markup multiplier, so a customer gets working billing on day one with one number instead of a rate-card builder.
Basis
direct: web/internal/billing/src/tiers.ts implements tiered distribution ("accumulating from lowest to highest with null centsPerUnit = free"); subscriptions.ts encodes Unkey's own $0.0001/verification, $0.00001/ratelimit tiers. "No API to define or apply custom pricing to keys, identities, or APIs for end-user billing." [verifier: sound; cost-plus variant is a reasonable extension]
Rationale
The rating logic is the part everyone else sells as their core product — and Unkey already built and battle-tests it on its own invoices. The gap is only that pricing is hardcoded to Unkey-as-seller. Generalizing one proven component removes the top reason customers fall back to spreadsheets.
Downsides
Has no home until Idea 2 exists (a rate card needs a subject to attach to). Pricing-model surface area grows fast (currency, rounding, proration, minimums). The cost-plus default is only lightly evidenced beyond "the engine exists."
4.

Zero-liability usage-export & invoice-draft API · recommended first ship

invoicing & collectionConfidence 85%Complexity: Low–Medium
Description
A POST /v2/billing.exportUsage endpoint returning per-identity billable quantities (JSON/CSV) for a period — and, one step further, a pre-computed Stripe invoice-draft payload (rated via Idea 3) the customer posts to their own Stripe. Unkey never touches money. Optionally pair it with an authoritative-counts webhook the customer diffs against their own logs (a reconciliation-tolerance feed), turning Unkey's number into the trusted source of truth. Reuses the exact register.go + zen.Route + OpenAPI pattern that v2/analytics.getVerifications already uses.
Basis
direct: no data-export surfaces exist (no CSV/JSON download of billable usage, no bulk export routes); route pattern confirmed at svc/api/routes/register.go (RegisterRoute + zen.Route) with the analytics handler as a working template. [verifier: sound — lowest-risk, well-grounded MVP]
Rationale
This is the most literal answer to the complaint ("they have to extract all the data and then bill their own users") — and it carries none of the compliance/liability weight of moving money. It ships fast on the Idea 1 rollup, de-risks the bigger bet, and is a clean predecessor to Connect (Idea 5).
Downsides
Leaves the actual billing to the customer — it removes the export toil but not the "run your own Stripe integration" step. Less differentiated than merchant-of-record. The invoice-draft half still depends on rate cards (Idea 3) to be more than raw counts.
5.

Stripe Connect — bill your end-users on your behalf

invoicing & collectionConfidence 60%Complexity: High
Description
Add Stripe Connect so a customer links their own Stripe account and Unkey posts meter events / draft invoices directly to it (on_behalf_of / destination), keyed to the end-user's Stripe customer resolved from the identity's billing profile (Idea 2). The monthly cron and meter-posting shape from deploybilling / billingmeter/stripe.go is the conceptual template; Unkey becomes the platform biller, its customer stays merchant-of-record.
Basis
direct: billingmeter/stripe.go already posts month-to-date meter totals keyed by StripeCustomerID; "No evidence of Stripe Connect (on_behalf_of/marketplace billing)." external: Stripe Connect / AWS Marketplace metered SaaS as the platform-as-biller pattern. [verifier: weak on the reuse claim — Connect needs a different auth/account model (connected-account tokens, Stripe-Account header), not just parameterizing the existing customer-id field]
Rationale
This is the differentiated version of the whole feature: the customer stops running any billing integration at all. It reuses the metering pipeline's customer-ID-parametric shape and is the natural v2 once the export API (Idea 4) proves demand.
Downsides
Connect is a materially larger build than the pusher reuse implies — different auth model, KYC/onboarding, and payout flows. It is a strategic and liability decision (does Unkey want to sit near other people's money) as much as an engineering one, and warrants explicit go/no-go.
6.

Embeddable end-user usage & billing surfaces

surfacing & self-serveConfidence 65%Complexity: Medium–High
Description
Turn the placeholder end-user Portal and existing usage-chart primitives into real per-identity billing surfaces, sourced from the Idea 1 rollup. Decomposes into distinct pieces (worth scoping separately): (a) fill the branded Portal's "coming soon" analytics with per-identity usage/cost; (b) an embeddable usage/cost widget the customer drops into their own app; (c) a v2/billing.getStatement endpoint with opening/closing/itemized lines the end-user can dispute; (d) usage.threshold_crossed / invoice.ready webhooks.
Basis
direct: Portal analytics is a placeholder ("Analytics dashboard coming soon"); usage-sparkline.tsx / usage-popover.tsx primitives already exist but are unwired to per-identity data; session carries ExternalID but no billing/usage fields; "no webhook events for customers — webhooks are internal only." [verifier: sound, but this is really 4 ideas — de-bundle before scheduling]
Rationale
Customers billing their end-users need those end-users to see the usage that justifies the bill. An embeddable widget spares every customer from building the same reporting UI, and the branded Portal shell + chart components already exist — the missing piece is wiring the billing numbers in.
Downsides
Bundles four different scopes/owners (UI, embeddable component, API, webhooks) with different risk profiles. Embedding into customer apps raises auth-scoping and branding surface area. Lower leverage than the data/pricing foundation it sits on.
7.

Immutable signed billing snapshots for audit & disputes

metering & aggregationConfidence 70%Complexity: Medium
Description
At each period close, freeze the per-identity rollup (Idea 1) into an immutable, hash-signed billing_period_ledger row with long retention, decoupling billing evidence from the 90-day raw-event TTL. Late-arriving events land as a visible adjustment line in the next period rather than silently restating a closed one — the toll-road / bank-statement reconciliation model. Customers (and their end-users) bill from a snapshot they can independently verify.
Basis
direct: key_verifications_raw_v2 has a 90-day TTL (schema 001); per-month view retains 3 years; billingmeter/stripe.go deliberately sets no event-level idempotency key and uses absolute month-to-date ("last") totals — note this is an INTENTIONAL design (a stable id would make Stripe hard-400 on re-runs), so this idea adds an audit ledger, it does not "fix" a bug. [verifier: sound — refuted the "idempotency is missing" framing; this snapshot idea stands corrected on that basis]
Rationale
The moment real money moves off Unkey's data, disputes and audits become a compliance requirement. Unkey's raw retention is tuned for observability, not finance — a frozen, signed monthly snapshot turns that limitation into an auditability guarantee competitors don't offer, and prevents the "show me the line items from 4 months ago" support escalation.
Downsides
Added storage and a period-close job to operate and monitor. Signing/verification is only worth it once customers actually bill on the data. Adjustment-line semantics (how late is too late) need a clear, communicated policy.
